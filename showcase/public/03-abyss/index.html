SOUNDING

A field log of one descent — surface to trench floor, 10,935 metres. The log reads downward. The only way out is through.

ZONE 01 · EPIPELAGIC · 0–200 m

Where the light still argues

Day 1 · 06:41. We slipped under on a flat calm, and the light came down with us like a curious animal — long blades of it swinging through the green, stitching plankton into brief constellations. Everything here is traffic. Copepods rising, carbon falling, a billion small elevators running both directions at once. This bright lobby is the sea’s entire economy, and it is thinner, top to bottom, than our morning’s sail was long.

SPECIMEN 19‑A · COPEPOD · Calanus finmarchicus A rice-grain crustacean that outweighs, in aggregate, every human being on Earth. Its daily climb to the surface and back is, at scale, a marathon swum before breakfast.

ZONE 02 · MESOPELAGIC · 200–1,000 m

The last rumor of blue

Day 1 · 09:12. Blue is the last color to surrender, and at four hundred metres it is less a light than a rumor of one. The fish here have decided a rumor is enough to hide from: mirror flanks, hatchet bellies, eyes grown wide as portholes. Our sonar keeps reporting a seafloor that is not there — a false bottom of packed small bodies, millions deep, that rises every dusk to graze beneath the stars and sinks again before dawn. The largest migration on Earth runs nightly through this room, and sells no tickets.

SPECIMEN 19‑F · LANTERNFISH · Myctophum punctatum Likely the most numerous vertebrate on the planet. Belly photophores tuned to the exact blue of falling daylight erase its silhouette from below.

ZONE 03 · BATHYPELAGIC · 1,000–4,000 m

Light becomes speech

Day 2 · the clock stops mattering. Past a thousand metres the sun is a memory the water no longer keeps, and our floodlights feel like shouting in a library. When we cut them, the dark is not empty — it breathes. Jellies pulse past like slow lungs. Every eddy of our wake wakes an answering ring of blue sparks. We have stopped thinking of light as something to see by. Down here it is grammar: courtship, alarm, ambush, all spoken in the same cold syllable.

SPECIMEN 19‑K · ATOLLA JELLYFISH · Atolla wyvillei When seized, it spins a wheel of running blue light: a scream in photons — a burglar alarm that invites something larger to eat whatever is eating it.

ZONE 04 · ABYSSOPELAGIC · 4,000–6,000 m

The weather here is snow

Day 3. The snow never stops. What falls past our windows has been falling for weeks — shed carapaces, dust of shell, the slow ash of that bright economy four kilometres above. It settles on the widest habitat this planet owns, and the least visited; we hold better maps of the far side of the Moon. Four hundred atmospheres now. The hull ticks as it agrees to the pressure, one rivet at a time. Send a ping, and the water answers with shapes it never volunteers.

SPECIMEN 19‑P · DUMBO OCTOPUS · Grimpoteuthis The deepest-living octopus known. It does not jet; it rows, flying slowly on two ear-like fins, calm at pressures where steel thinks twice.

ZONE 05 · HADAL · 6,000–10,935 m

The floor arrives quietly

Day 4 · bottom. 10,935 metres. The floor arrives quietly — a pale ooze older than our species, printed with the tracks of animals no one has named. At the rim of our lights something waits with a lantern of its own, patient as geology, hunting by hospitality. We came down expecting silence and found a language instead. So we do the polite thing. We turn our lights off.

SPECIMEN 19‑Z · ANGLERFISH · Melanocetus Hunts by trust: one bacterial lamp held above a patient mouth. In the trench, hope is a lure.

End of log. We were never the first lantern here — only the newest.
