Foreword

The Folium Society exists because of a suspicion, voiced one wet November evening and never successfully put down: that a plant is not a shape but a rule. A hedge-botanist presses what the season gives; we press the season itself. Each specimen in this fascicle is a small grammar — an instruction for stems, a habit for leaves, a patience for flowers — and what you see on the plate is that grammar caught in the act of being obeyed.

Nothing here was drawn. When a plate comes into view its specimen grows for you, as it first grew for us, vein by vein and strictly in order: roots, stem, leaf, and flower last, since flowers must be earned. Should you doubt a plant, hold its seed number; sow it again on the propagation plate at the end of this fascicle and it will return, leaf for leaf, to argue its own case.

E. Marchmont, Keeper of the Plates
Given at the Society’s rooms, Candlemas MMXXVI

The Plates

Six further gatherings, pressed in the order they were walked to. Each grows as it enters your view, and will grow again if you touch its specimen — the Society cannot stop them.

On the Gathering of These Specimens

Our collectors do not walk hedgerows. They walk parameter space — that endless, unfenced country where every coordinate is a possible plant. An expedition begins with a bearing (you would call it a seed number) and proceeds by strict rule: so much curl to a frond, so many degrees between one leaf and the next, flowers only when the stem has earned them. The collector’s whole art is in choosing where to stop walking.

The country is larger than any flora yet written. Ninety-nine thousand bearings lie within reach of the plate below, and every one of them grows a plant that has always been there, waiting to be walked to. We have gathered seven. The rest we leave to you, with the Society’s compliments and a trowel.

Tabula Seminalis · The Propagation Plate

Sow a seed and watch its accession grow. Every seed yields one plant and one plant only — the number is the plant’s true name. A reroll is a new accession.

Fasciculus II Is in the Ground

The Society issues its fascicles as the algorithm flowers, which is to say: on no schedule a printer would tolerate. The second gathering — mosses, seeded thistles, and a water-plant that insists on growing downward — is already pressed into parameter space and waiting to be walked to.
